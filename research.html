Job Market Paper

Job Market Paper
"Optimal Dynamic Selection Under Costly Evaluation: Evidence from Graduate Admissions"

This paper uses graduate admissions to study dynamic selection under costly evaluation. With a synthetic data protocol to protect applicants' privacy, I relate acceptance to measures of success, such as post-program job placements. I also develop structural models of an admissions committee's decision problem along with statistical tests of whether its decisions are optimal; that is, whether it accepts the applicants with the highest success probabilities. While the economics graduate admissions literature from Krueger and Wu (2000) to Bai, Esche, MacLeod, and Shi (2022) has identified determinants of acceptance and success in application files, no paper has estimated a structural model of the committee's problem or tested whether committees optimize. I show that reduced-form comparisons of the determinants of acceptance and success cannot test optimality because the mechanics of multi-stage evaluation can make those determinants differ even when the committee optimizes.

Instead, I develop and estimate structural models in increasing order of complexity. I start with two-round static, dynamic, and myopic models with a first-stage filter based on objective variables such as GRE scores, and a second-stage evaluation incorporating subjective variables such as those from textual processing of recommendation letters. I then present three-round models of the waitlist process where the committee gives early offers to likely matriculants to avoid losing strong applicants to competing universities, along with infinite-horizon multiyear extensions that incorporate matriculation and diversity dynamics. Kolmogorov-Smirnov tests of deviation gains reject the null of optimality when the committee misvalues applicant characteristics, such as discriminating against a demographic, but not when it optimizes. This framework applies to any institutional setting where decision-makers select from available options under uncertainty and evaluation costs, such as hiring and clinical trials. The synthetic data protocol also serves as a template for researchers conducting structural estimation on confidential data they cannot directly access.
